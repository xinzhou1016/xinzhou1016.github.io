Network Effect and Platform Competition

Evidence from Online Dating Markets

Motivation

The canonical view

  • Platforms “powered by network effects”: more users $\Rightarrow$ value $\Rightarrow$ tipping
  • Underlies both platform-efficiency narratives and antitrust concerns
  • Treats network effects as a single platform-level property

But the relevant network is local

  • Dating, ride-hail, classifieds: utility depends on the local matching pool, not global size (Tucker 2008; Candogan & Bloch 2013)
  • How local conditions shape network-effect strength is still empirically open
  • Prior work: positive shocks; we exploit a negative regulatory shock

Our setting

May 2019: Tantan removed from major Chinese app stores for ~2 months. National policy, but pre-shock intensity varies across 100 cities. Existing users retained access; rivals unaffected.

Four Research Questions

  1. Propagation. How does a regulatory removal affect user participation on the focal platform and its rivals — across new users and existing engagement?
  2. Heterogeneity. How do local market characteristics shape the pattern of these responses across cities?
  3. Magnitude. What is the underlying strength of local network effects — how much do users value platform size as a structural preference parameter?
  4. Market structure. How does network-effect strength translate into market concentration?

Descriptive Evidence III: 30-Day Retention

Key insight

Existing Tantan users retained access — yet they disengaged. The platform became less valuable as the matching market shrank.

Dashed line = May 1, 2019. Tantan retention ↓ sharply; Yidui ↑ gradually; Jiayuan no clear break.

DAU Decomposition: New vs. Existing Users

Goal: separately identify responses on the extensive margin (new users) and intensive margin (incumbent engagement).

Rolling 30-day window

$\mathrm{NewDAU}^{(30)}_t = \displaystyle\sum_{c=t-30}^{t} N_c \cdot r(t-c)$

where $r(a)$ is the retention rate for users of age $a$.

$\mathrm{ExistingDAU}^{(30)}_t = \mathrm{DAU}_t - \mathrm{NewDAU}^{(30)}_t$

New DAU = users joined in past 30 days; Existing DAU = older cohorts.

Part II

Identification Strategy

Empirical Strategy: Bayesian Synthetic Control

Challenge: platform user bases are endogenous. Approach: exploit Tantan’s suspension as a quasi-exogenous shock; treatment effect $\tau_{jct} = Y_{jct} - \hat{Y}^{0}_{jct}$, summarized as ATT.

Synthetic control (Pang, Liu & Xu 2022)

  • Latent factor model flexibly matches pre-shock dynamics
  • Donor pool: non-dating apps + same app’s previous-year trajectory — plausibly unaffected by the shock
  • Treatment effect at app × city level

Identification of network effects

Existing users who retained access change behavior $\Rightarrow$ attributable to reduced network value, not supply restriction.

Robustness: Ruling Out Brand-Reputation Effects

Alternative interpretation: existing-user disengagement could reflect reputational damage from the takedown, not network shrinkage.

Placebo test setup (March 1, 2019)

  • By March 2019, critical coverage of Tantan’s inappropriate content was already circulating; no formal regulatory action yet
  • A slow-burn reputational channel should already be disengaging incumbents

Result

  • Re-estimate bpCausal with placebo treatment date $=$ March 1, 2019
  • Post-placebo ATT on existing-user DAU $= \mathbf{+4.9\%}$, indistinguishable from zero
  • vs. actual May 2019 ATT of $\mathbf{-15.3\%}$ on the same outcome — opposite sign, order of magnitude apart

Conclusion

A reputation channel that should have been acting by March 2019 leaves no detectable trace. The May 2019 disengagement traces to network shrinkage, not stigma.

Double-edged thick markets

  • Resilient new-user acquisition
  • More vulnerable existing-user engagement

Takeaway: strong pre-shock networks are both an asset and a liability.

Interpreting the Asymmetry: Stock vs. Flow

Why are thicker markets simultaneously more resilient (new users) and more vulnerable (existing users)? The two margins depend on different parts of the local matching market.

New-user margin: stock matters

  • Every incumbent is novel to a new user; thicker stock $\rightarrow$ joining still attractive
  • $\Rightarrow$ thicker markets more resilient on acquisition

Existing-user margin: flow matters

  • Incumbents have exhausted breadth $\rightarrow$ engagement depends on flow of new arrivals
  • Smaller % drop, but larger absolute drop (higher baseline); incumbents respond to absolute numbers
  • $\Rightarrow$ thicker markets more vulnerable on engagement
Part IV

Structural Model

  1. Magnitude. What is the underlying strength of local network effects — how much do users value platform size as a structural preference parameter?
  2. Market structure. How does network-effect strength translate into market concentration?

Structural Model: Setup

BLP demand model at the level of activity occasions.

$U_{ijct} = \alpha_j + \beta_{i,c}\log(\mathrm{MAU}_{j,c,t-1}) + \gamma^\top X_{jct} + \varepsilon_{ijct}$

Heterogeneous network preferences:

$\beta_{i,c} = \beta_0 + \eta^\top Z_c + \theta_{\mathrm{age}}\,\mathrm{age}_i + \sigma\,\nu_i, \quad \nu_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$

Heterogeneity sources

  • $Z_c$: log GDP per capita, density (centered)
  • Age: drawn from city Census distribution
  • $\sigma\,\nu_i$: unobserved taste shock on network value

Key modeling choices

  • DAU = usage share, not exclusive ownership
  • Outside option: non-usage + unobserved apps

Interpreting the Estimates

Mean network effect ($\hat{\beta}_0 = 0.598$)

  • 10% larger MAU $\Rightarrow$ log-odds rise by $\approx 0.057$
  • $\approx 5.7\%$ increase in usage probability for apps with small shares

City-level heterogeneity

  • Richer cities ($\hat{\eta}_1 > 0$): higher time cost $\Rightarrow$ greater value of a thicker matching market
  • Denser cities ($\hat{\eta}_2 < 0$): more offline alternatives reduce incremental value of online network size

Demographic heterogeneity ($\hat{\theta}_{\mathrm{age}} = -0.0018$)

  • Older users place less weight on platform size
  • Identified from Nevo-style age micro-moments (Tantan, Momo, Jiayuan, Soul)
Part V

Counterfactual Analysis

Counterfactual Methodology

Goal: use the structural model to evaluate how network-effect strength shapes equilibrium concentration.

Within-dating HHI (antitrust-standard normalization)

$\mathrm{HHI}_{ct} = \displaystyle\sum_{j\in\mathcal{J}_{ct}} \widetilde{s}_{jct}^{2}, \qquad \widetilde{s}_{jct} = \frac{\hat{s}_{jct}}{\sum_{k\in\mathcal{J}_{ct}} \hat{s}_{kct}}$

Anchored in observed data

  • Pre-shock (Jan–Apr 2019): mean HHI $= 0.535$
  • Shock (May–Jun 2019): $0.544$ ($+1.7\%$)
  • Post-shock (Jul–Dec 2019): $0.525$
  • Actual policy concentrated the market only modestly — Tantan’s existing users retained access

Three categories of scenarios

  • (i) Tantan removal (non-dominant): full May–Jun 2019 removal
  • (ii) Momo removal (dominant): per-city dominant-platform removal, full panel
  • (iii) Network-effect strength: vary the level $\beta_0$

Result: Dominant vs. Non-Dominant Asymmetry

(i) Removing Tantan (non-dominant) RAISES concentration

  • HHI $0.544 \to \mathbf{0.707}$ ($\mathbf{+30\%}$ in affected markets)
  • Removing a non-dominant platform concentrates the market

(ii) Removing Momo (dominant) CUTS concentration

  • HHI $0.531 \to \mathbf{0.355}$ ($\mathbf{-33\%}$): largest concentration-reducing intervention in our scenario set
  • Residual market fragments rather than concentrating around a single rival

The central counterfactual result

Removing a non-dominant platform concentrates the market; removing the dominant platform fragments it. The May 2019 Tantan policy, in its more aggressive limit, would have made the market more concentrated.

(iii) Network-Effect Strength Drives Concentration

Level of $\beta_0$

  • Doubling $\beta_0 = 0.598 \to 1.20$:
    HHI $0.531 \to \mathbf{0.754}$ ($\mathbf{+42\%}$)
  • Halving $\beta_0 \to 0.30$:
    HHI $\to 0.383$ ($-28\%$)
  • Comparable to non-dominant-platform removal: the structural primitive matters as much as the policy lever

Takeaway

Stronger network effects $\Rightarrow$ substantially more concentrated equilibria.
Part VI

Discussion & Conclusion

Discussion & Conclusion

Takeaway

Network effects are local: in thicker markets, new-user acquisition is more resilient to inflow shocks but existing-user engagement is more vulnerable — and stronger network effects translate into substantially more concentrated equilibria.

For platform managers

  • Network strength is a city-level asset $\Rightarrow$ city-specific strategy
  • Older segments are less network-sensitive $\Rightarrow$ age-based niche positioning is sustainable

For competition policy

  • HHI direction depends on local pre-shock dominance: dominant $\to -33\%$; non-dominant $\to +30\%$
  • Network-effect strength is itself the structural lever that matters

Appendix: Instrument Validity

Exclusion restriction

  • Suspension driven by Cyberspace Administration of China content-regulation campaign
  • Unrelated to local dating demand conditions
  • Residual regulatory sentiment absorbed by shared time fixed effects

First-stage diagnostics

  • Joint Wald $F = 33.1$ on the six instruments $\{\mathrm{IV}^T, \mathrm{IV}^R\} \times \{1, \log\mathrm{GDP}_c, \mathrm{dens}_c\}$
  • Comfortably above the Stock–Yogo critical value of $\approx 26$
  • Joint $F = 201.1$ on the two bare instruments without city-characteristic interactions

Pre-trend check

  • Dynamic treatment effects for existing Tantan users near zero in pre-shock period
  • Supports parallel-trends / no-anticipation assumptions
